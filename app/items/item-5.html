Scientific Knowledge

The original wonder cabinets featured objects of significant scientific importance, and led to greater public understanding of scientific practice and discovery. One of the most famous collections was created by 17th century Danish doctor, Ole Worm. It contained hundreds of items of natural history carefully arranged in his own home. Upon his death, a detailed catalogue was published as Museum Wormianum, the starting point for his teaching on science, philosophy and natural history.

In March 2012, Elise Andrew set up a Facebook group called IFLScience where she curated and commented upon the ‘bizarre facts and cool pictures’ she regularly came across. Just like the original cabinets of wonder, the site was intended for ‘a few dozen of [Andrew’s] friends’ but drew much wider attention as its relevance was recognised by the wider community (today standing at more than 20 million likes).

IFLScience shows the extent to which this format continues to resonate with the public and amateur scientist. The curation of scientific content via Facebook, the digital wonder cabinet, has enabled Andrew to create a career in science communication. Just like Ole Worm’s curation and discussion of science hundreds of years previously, Andrew’s IFLScience page has brought scientific discoveries and curiosities to new audiences.
